Dynamic Routing for Marine Debris Collection

Optimizing vessel paths in dynamic ocean environments with drifting plastic hotspots

üìç Problem Setting

Marine debris tends to accumulate in oceanic convergence zones. These zones contain floating plastic patches‚Äîcalled hotspots‚Äîthat vary in size and drift with ocean currents.

Objective: Maximize collected plastic value within a fixed time horizon by choosing which hotspots to visit, and in what order.

üìê Mathematical Model

The vessel routing problem is formalized as a dynamic Team Orienteering Problem with Moving Targets (TOP-MT). The objective is to maximize the total collected value within a fixed time horizon.

Score Function

\[ \text{Score}_i = \alpha \cdot \left(1 - \frac{d_i}{d_{\max}}\right) + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \left(\frac{v_i}{v_{\max}}\right) \]
  • \( d_i \): distance between the vessel and hotspot \(i\)
  • \( v_i \): value of hotspot \(i\)
  • \( \alpha \in [0,1] \): trade-off parameter
  • \( d_{\max}, v_{\max} \): maximum distance/value in current evaluation window

Optimization Objective

The optimization problem is:

\[ \max \sum_{i \in S} v_i \cdot x_i \]

Subject to:

  • \( \sum_{i \in S} t_i \cdot x_i \leq T \): total travel and service time within time budget \(T\)
  • \( x_i \in \{0, 1\} \): binary variable indicating if hotspot \(i\) is visited

Current & Movement Model

The movement of vessels and hotspots is influenced by ocean currents. These are represented as vector fields \( \vec{u}(x, y, t) \), \( \vec{v}(x, y, t) \) over the 2D sea surface.

At each time step \(t\), the position \((x_i, y_i)\) of a hotspot is updated as:

\[ x_i(t+1) = x_i(t) + u(x_i, y_i, t) \cdot \Delta t \] \[ y_i(t+1) = y_i(t) + v(x_i, y_i, t) \cdot \Delta t \]

Vessels are subject to similar drift, but also have their own propulsion vector added in the planning logic.

üß† Greedy Routing Logic

  1. Compute score for all available hotspots
  2. Select the one with the highest score
  3. Travel to and collect it
  4. Re-evaluate and repeat

Changing \( \alpha \) leads to very different behaviors:

  • \( \alpha = 1 \): proximity only ‚Üí short paths, but low value
  • \( \alpha = 0 \): value only ‚Üí long detours, high risk
  • Balanced \( \alpha \): optimized trade-off
